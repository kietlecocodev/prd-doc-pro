# Good Example: Target Users / Personas (Mobile Health App)

**Score: 10/10** — Specific, contextual, distinct, actionable

---

## Target Users

### Persona 1: Maria — Active Health Tracker
- **Role**: 32-year-old marketing manager who runs 3x/week and tracks nutrition
- **Device**: iPhone 14, always on latest iOS, Apple Watch Series 8
- **Usage pattern**: Opens app 4-5x/day — morning weigh-in, post-workout log, meal tracking, evening review
- **Primary goal**: See all health data in one place without switching between 4 apps (Apple Health, MyFitnessPal, Strava, Sleep Cycle)
- **Main frustration**: "I have data everywhere but no single view of my health. I want to see how my sleep affects my running performance without doing the math myself."
- **Technical comfort**: Advanced — comfortable with integrations, API connections, data exports
- **Willingness to pay**: High — currently pays for 3 separate apps ($25/month total). Would consolidate for a single premium app at $12-15/month.
- **What success looks like for Maria**: She opens one app instead of four. She sees a weekly insight like "Your runs are 12% faster after 7+ hours of sleep."

### Persona 2: David — Reluctant Health Beginner
- **Role**: 55-year-old accountant whose doctor recommended tracking blood pressure and daily steps
- **Device**: Samsung Galaxy A54 (mid-range Android), no wearable
- **Usage pattern**: Opens app 1x/day — evening blood pressure entry. Forgets 2-3 days/week.
- **Primary goal**: Meet his doctor's request to track BP and steps so he has data for his next appointment
- **Main frustration**: "Health apps are designed for fitness people. I just need to type in two numbers and maybe see a chart for my doctor. I don't need calories, macros, or workout plans."
- **Technical comfort**: Beginner — struggles with app permissions, confused by sync settings, prefers large text
- **Willingness to pay**: Low — will use free tier. May convert to paid if the app helps him at his doctor visit.
- **What success looks like for David**: He remembers to log BP daily (push notification at 8pm). He exports a PDF for his doctor showing 3 months of BP trends.

### Persona 3: Dr. Nguyen — Reviewing Physician
- **Role**: 44-year-old primary care physician who wants patients to self-track between visits
- **Device**: iPhone 15 Pro (personal), hospital-issued iPad
- **Usage pattern**: Reviews patient-shared data 2x/week during appointment prep (5 minutes per patient)
- **Primary goal**: See patient health trends at a glance without asking patients to remember numbers
- **Main frustration**: "Patients show me screenshots of random apps. I need a clean PDF or read-only dashboard with BP, weight, and activity trends over 90 days."
- **Technical comfort**: Intermediate — comfortable with EMR systems, wants minimal friction
- **Willingness to pay**: N/A — doesn't pay. But recommends apps to patients (30+ patients/month). Acts as a growth channel.
- **What success looks like for Dr. Nguyen**: Receives a shared link from a patient, opens it in browser, sees a clean 90-day health summary in 10 seconds.

## Why This Is Good

| Criterion | How It's Met |
|-----------|-------------|
| Specific names and context | Maria (32, marketer, runner), David (55, accountant, doctor's orders), Dr. Nguyen (44, physician) |
| Distinct personas | Power user vs beginner vs professional reviewer — each has fundamentally different needs |
| Device context | Specific phone models + wearable status — impacts feature scope and performance targets |
| Usage pattern | Frequency and timing specified — informs push notification strategy and offline needs |
| Frustration as quote | First-person quotes make the pain tangible and prevent abstract persona descriptions |
| Willingness to pay | Informs monetization strategy — which personas convert to paid? |
| Success definition | Concrete outcomes, not abstract "is satisfied" — verifiable in user testing |
| Mobile-specific | Device type, wearable integration, push notification strategy per persona |
